Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

pandas · descriptive stats · missing value audit

🔍 What is this?
Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the very first step — it's like opening a box you've never seen before and taking inventory of what's inside. Before writing any code to predict things, a data scientist needs to understand the raw data: How many rows are there? Are any values missing? What does the age distribution look like? Are there obvious patterns?

In this case, we found that 77% of the "deck" column was missing (so we removed it), and that age was missing for ~20% of passengers (so we filled those gaps with the average age). We also noticed right away that younger passengers tended to survive more — that kind of insight guides everything that comes next.

Data Cleaning & Preprocessing

sklearn · LabelEncoder · StandardScaler · imputation

🧹 What is this?
Data Cleaning & Preprocessing is turning messy, real-world data into something a computer can actually learn from. Raw data is almost never perfect — it has missing values, text categories (like "male"/"female") that computers can't do math on, and numbers on wildly different scales (age goes 0–80, fare goes 0–500).

We fixed all of this: filled in missing ages with the median, converted text to numbers (e.g. "female" → 0, "male" → 1), and scaled everything so no single column dominates just because its numbers are bigger. Think of it like prepping ingredients before cooking — the better the prep, the better the result.

Data Visualisation

matplotlib · seaborn · correlation heatmap · grouped bar charts

📊 What is this?
Data Visualisation is turning numbers into pictures so patterns become obvious at a glance. A table with 891 rows tells you almost nothing. A chart that shows "women survived at 74% vs men at 19%" tells a story instantly.

The bar charts show survival rates broken down by ticket class and gender. The correlation heatmap is a grid showing how strongly each column relates to survival — the darker the colour, the stronger the connection. For example, sex and passenger class have a strong negative correlation with survival, meaning being male or in 3rd class significantly reduced your chances.

Machine Learning – Random Forest Classifier

sklearn · confusion matrix · ROC curve · classification report

🤖 What is this?
Machine Learning is teaching a computer to make predictions by learning from examples — without being explicitly programmed with rules. We used a Random Forest, which builds hundreds of decision trees (each one asking questions like "Was this passenger female? Was their fare above $50?") and combines their answers for a final prediction.

We trained the model on 80% of the data, then tested it on the remaining 20% it had never seen — this tells us how well it generalises to new passengers. The confusion matrix shows exactly how many predictions were right vs wrong. The ROC curve measures how good the model is at distinguishing survivors from non-survivors — the closer to the top-left corner, the better. Our AUC of 0.866 means the model is significantly better than random guessing.

Feature Importance Analysis

RandomForest · feature_importances_ · Gini impurity

⚖️ What is this?
Feature Importance answers the question: "Which pieces of information actually mattered to the model's predictions?" After training, the Random Forest tells us how much each column contributed to getting predictions right. A longer bar = that factor had more influence on whether someone survived.

The results are striking: Sex was the single most important factor (36.9%), followed by the fare paid (19%) and age (15.8%). This aligns with the historical reality of the Titanic — "women and children first" was the evacuation priority. Interestingly, which port someone boarded from barely mattered at all (2.9%), confirming it's not a meaningful predictor.
